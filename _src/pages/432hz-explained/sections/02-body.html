What a tuning standard is

Instruments have to agree on a reference pitch to play together, and the reference has moved around for centuries — orchestras in different eras and cities tuned A anywhere from the low 400s to the 450s. In the twentieth century, A = 440 Hz became the international convention, mostly for standardization rather than any acoustic magic. 432 Hz is one of the older-style lower references, revived as an alternative. The difference is real but small: about a third of a semitone, which most listeners cannot identify without a side-by-side comparison.

Why some people love it

Fans of 432 tuning describe it as warmer, softer, more settled — and for slow, sustained music like a sound bath, a slightly lower pitch center genuinely can read as mellower. Some facilitators tune their bowls this way and care about it deeply, the way a guitarist cares about a particular amp. That is a legitimate aesthetic preference, and a session tuned to 432 can be lovely.

What the evidence says

The stronger claims — that 432 Hz is "the frequency of the universe," matches the earth's resonance, restructures water or DNA, or heals where 440 cannot — do not hold up. The physical claims are inaccurate on their own terms (the earth's Schumann resonance is about 7.8 Hz, not 432, and octave math does not rescue the connection), and the handful of small studies comparing 432 to 440 playback have found, at most, tiny differences that have not replicated into anything solid. When a 432 session feels wonderful, the honest explanation is the one that needs no numerology: an hour of stillness and slow, resonant sound is deeply relaxing at any tuning. More on that in what the science actually says.

Is 432 Hz scientifically proven to heal?

No. There is no reliable evidence that 432 Hz tuning has healing effects that other tunings lack. The relaxation people feel at a 432 session is real — it just comes from the session, not the number.

Does it matter which tuning my sound bath uses?

Practically, no. Choose a session by facilitator, instruments, size, and setting — the things that actually shape the hour. If a listing on the calendar mentions 432 Hz, read it as a note about the facilitator's craft and taste, not a stronger therapeutic promise.

My facilitator swears by 432. Should that put me off?

Not by itself. Plenty of skilled, caring facilitators hold beliefs about tuning that outrun the evidence; the craft is in the playing and the room they hold, and you are free to enjoy the session without adopting the framework. If the claims tip into medical territory — promises to treat illness — that is the flag worth heeding, whatever the tuning.
